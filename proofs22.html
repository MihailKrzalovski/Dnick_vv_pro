Direct Proof

The simplest (from a logic perspective) style of proof is a direct proof. Often all that is required to prove something is a systematic explanation of what everything means. Direct proofs are especially useful when proving implications. The general format to prove P→QP→Q is this:

Assume P.P. Explain, explain, …, explain. Therefore Q.Q.

Proof by Contradiction

There might be statements which really cannot be rephrased as implications. For example, “√22 is irrational.” In this case, it is hard to know where to start. What can we assume? Well, say we want to prove the statement P.P. What if we could prove that ¬P→Q¬P→Q where QQ was false? If this implication is true, and QQ is false, what can we say about ¬P?¬P? It must be false as well, which makes PP true!

This is why proof by contradiction works. If we can prove that ¬P¬P leads to a contradiction, then the only conclusion is that ¬P¬P is false, so PP is true. That's what we wanted to prove. In other words, if it is impossible for PP to be false, PP must be true.
